Reading the mechanistic model

1. One physics, every step

Crowding enters through scaled-particle excluded volume (μ_ex), scale-dependent viscosity, and enzyme-class catalytic shifts. The same excluded volume that stabilises a duplex also tightens enzyme binding; the same viscosity that slows the polymerase slows the exonuclease. Nothing is fit per-constant — it all flows from φ, crowder radius and species sizes.

2. The oscillation engine

The shared exonuclease (ttRecJ) is a conserved, saturable resource. A crowd of predators swamps it, so a surge protects itself — that self-feeding is what makes the rhythm. Crowding that slows the exonuclease (exo inhibition) is the strongest lever on whether the rhythm survives.

3. PEG vs Ficoll — the headline

Crowder radius enters through z = r/R_c. Small-radius PEG excludes far more volume per unit φ and has a real semidilute mesh, so it crosses the Hopf boundary and kills the oscillation near ~10–12% w/v. Big, compact Ficoll is much milder and keeps oscillating. Watch the sweep: that divergence at equal φ is the falsifiable claim.

4. Why a full network

The two-variable read-out is for intuition only. The quantitative predictions come from integrating all seven species, because prey and predator are transiently held in the complexes — the bare reduction is too fragile to reproduce the period or the Hopf point.

⚠ Coefficients are hypotheses for calibration. All Ficoll-400 numbers are extrapolations (no primary Ficoll-400 datum in the surveyed literature), and the exonuclease anchor is an Exo I number — ttRecJ activity vs crowder is the single highest-value wet-lab measurement. The dilute limit (C=0) reproduces the validated paper cycle.
