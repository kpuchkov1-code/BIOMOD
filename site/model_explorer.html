1. The chase runs late

Predators are slow to respond. By the time many have built up, the prey has already overgrown. The predators overshoot and crash the prey too far; starved, the predators crash too; then the few surviving prey boom again, even higher than before. That lag turns a steady balance into an endless back-and-forth chase.

2. Why the waves grow, then settle

A wave's size is just how far the numbers swing from the calm balance point. We start close to that point, so the first swing is small. Each cycle overshoots a little more, so the swings grow, until natural ceilings cap them. On the phase portrait this is the trajectory spiralling outward to its final loop.

3. The real engine

The killer enzyme can only work so fast. When predators are few it removes a big fraction; when they spike they swamp it, so only a small fraction die. A crowd of predators is protected just by being a crowd, so a surge feeds itself instead of being corrected. That self-feeding is what makes the balance unstable and the waves self-sustaining.

4. The on/off knobs

Template G sets the richness: too little starves the predator, too much damps to a flat line (waves live roughly 80 to 200 nM). rec and Km,P control the "safety in numbers" effect: set rec to 0 and the waves vanish. Crowding nudges exactly these knobs, which is what your study probes.
